Case Study

Rebuilding Revenue Infrastructure
DTC Skincare Brand · Shopify + Klaviyo

This brand had demand, but too much growth was being carried by paid traffic. AOV was low, deliverability was weak, subscription adoption was underperforming, and retention was not yet functioning like a real growth lever. I rebuilt the lifecycle and site foundation to improve trust, repeat purchase, subscription uptake, and long-term customer value.

The Situation
  • Growth heavily dependent on Meta and influencer spend
  • AOV ~$18 limiting LTV potential
  • Deliverability issues causing poor inbox placement
  • No structured loyalty or retention engine
  • Weak subscription adoption, churn trending up
My Diagnosis

After auditing the funnel, the issue wasn't traffic. It was systems alignment, subscription UX, and lifecycle sequencing — none of which were designed to drive repeat behavior. Retention wasn't a growth lever. Paid had to carry everything.

Execution
  • Fixed deliverability via SPF, DKIM, DMARC + copy/cadence/segmentation cleanup
  • Migrated from Mailchimp → Klaviyo for cleaner lifecycle architecture
  • Rebuilt Shopify: nav, PDPs, premium layout, and subscription UX end-to-end
  • Implemented core flows: welcome, post-purchase, replenishment, winback
  • Coordinated packaging, pricing reset, and claims for premium positioning
  • Launched loyalty + insert strategy to drive reviews and repeat purchases
Outcome
  • Email/SMS became highest converting channel, outpacing paid ads
  • Unsubscribe rate dropped to near-zero after lifecycle restructure
  • Subscription adoption improved ~5 points through UX + framing changes
  • New haircare vertical launched on separate Shopify instance with full lifecycle stack

Audit Work

Klaviyo Lifecycle Audit
Haven · Premium Home Textiles DTC Brand

Haven had a real lifecycle foundation — flows generating meaningful revenue, checkout recovery performing at 3× benchmark. But the account was leaving significant opportunity on the table: opens without clicks, acquisition optimized for volume over purchase-readiness, and a post-purchase sequence that assumed too much and built too little confidence.

I audited the full Klaviyo account against peer benchmarks and surfaced four prioritized findings with clear, sequenced recommendations.

Bottom Line

The biggest gains come from improving subscriber quality-to-journey fit, strengthening post-purchase lifecycle depth, and making campaigns and cart messaging more relevant and confidence-building.

01
High Priority
Welcome Softness Is Tied to Source Quality, Not Flow Structure

Opens were healthy but clicks and revenue lagged significantly behind benchmark. After reviewing the flow, the creative was stronger than it first appeared — the real issue was upstream: mixed-intent subscriber entry from incentive-led acquisition diluting welcome efficiency.

Rec: Source-aware welcome analysis; branch mixed-intent entrants differently; treat acquisition quality as the larger lever.
02
High Priority
Acquisition Optimized for Volume Over Purchase-Readiness

Top opt-in sources were dominated by a "Win a Free Product" quiz and sale-based fullscreen forms — effective for list growth but feeding curiosity-driven subscribers into the same path as purchase-ready ones.

Rec: Review acquisition quality by source; give quiz/promo entrants a differentiated early journey with stronger education before conversion pressure increases.
03
Medium-High
Post-Delivery Underuses a High-Value Retention Moment

Post-delivery emails got the highest open rate (61.7%) but the lowest revenue per recipient ($0.96). The sequence was fragmented — support, survey, cross-sell, community, promo, and referral with no clear arc. One email assumed all buyers had a pet, creating an immediate "is this for me?" moment right when confidence should be peaking.

Rec: Refocus post-delivery around a confident, inclusive progression — reinforce the purchase, provide ownership education, inspire, then introduce secondary actions.
04
Medium-High
Campaigns and Cart Recovery Need Confidence, Not Hesitation Language

Campaign open rate beat peers (51.4%) but click rate was rated Poor (0.31% vs. 0.95% peer). People were opening out of habit — not because content felt relevant. "On the fence?" language appeared across flows, naming and reinforcing hesitation rather than building confidence toward a decision.

Rec: Replace hesitation-forward language with confidence-forward framing. Activate behavioral segments more intentionally; bring PDP-level confidence cues into the email itself.
